Figurative Language Commonly Found in Latin

1. Allegory

A narrative or description that conveys a hidden meaning, often moral or political.

2. Alliteration

The repetition of initial consonant sounds in a series of words

"Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers"

3. Anaphora

The repetition of a word/phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences.

4. Anastrophe

Inversion of word order

5. Aposiopesis

A sudden breaking off in speech, leaving a thought unfinished

"If you say that again, I'll—"

6. Apostrophe

A direct address to an absent person, object, or abstract idea

"O Death, where is thy sting?"

7. Asyndeton

The omission of conjunctions between clauses for effect

"I came, I saw, I conquered"

8. Chiasmus

A rhetorical device in which two clauses are structured in a reverse order

"Ask not what your country can do for you—ask what you can do for your country"

9. Ekphrasis

A vivid, detailed description of a work of art or a visual scene.

10. Ellipsis

The omission of words that are understood in context

"Some people prefer cats; others, dogs"

11. Enjambment

In poetry, the continuation of a sentence beyond the end of a line without a pause.

12. Hendiadys

The expression of a single idea using two nouns connected by "and" instead of an adjective-noun structure

"Sound and fury" instead of "furious sound"

13. Hyperbaton

The inversion of normal word order for emphasis

"This I must see"

14. Hyperbole

Exaggeration for effect

"I'm so hungry I could eat a horse"

15. Hysteron proteron

A reversal of the natural order of events

"Put on your shoes and socks"

16. Litotes

Understatement using double negatives or negating the opposite

"Not bad" meaning "good"

17. Metaphor

A direct comparison between two unlike things without using "like" or "as"

"Time is a thief"

18. Metonymy

Substituting the name of one thing with something closely related

"The White House issued a statement" instead of "The President issued a statement"

19. Onomatopoeia

A word that imitates a sound

"Buzz," "sizzle"

20. Personification

Giving human qualities to non-human things

"The wind whispered through the trees"

21. Pleonasm

The use of redundant words for emphasis

"Burning fire" or "free gift"

22. Polyptoton

Repetition of the same root word in different forms

"strong" and "strength"

23. Polysyndeton

The use of multiple conjunctions for emphasis

"We have ships and men and money and supplies"

24. Praeteritio

Calling attention to something by claiming to omit it

"I won't even mention his bad habits"

25. Prolepsis

Anticipating and answering objections in advance, or using a future event as if it has already occurred.

26. Prosopopoeia

A type of personification where an inanimate object or absent person is given speech.

27. Rhetorical question

A question asked for effect, not expecting an answer

"Isn't it obvious"

28. Simile

A comparison using "like" or "as"

"Brave as a lion"

29. Synchesis/Golden Line

A confused or interwoven word order (synchesis with a verb in the middle)

"Golden happy ring girl" instead of "happy golden-haired girl"

30. Synecdoche

Using a part to represent the whole

"All hands on deck" meaning sailors, not just their hands

31. Tmesis

The separation of a compound word into two parts, often for emphasis

"Abso-bloody-lutely"
"Quo quemque modo" = "quomodo quemque" aeneid VI.891

32. Transferred epithet

An adjective that grammatically modifies one noun but logically describes another

"Restless night" when it's the person who is restless, not the night

33. Tricolon crescens

A series of three elements in increasing intensity or size

"I came, I saw, I conquered"

34. Zeugma

A single word (often a verb) governing two or more words in different senses

"He stole my heart and my wallet"

Derivatives of Cases

Genitive

Partitive genitive

Used for the larger whole of which something is a part

"pars civitatis → part of the state"
All of the state is not partitive (it's not part of a whole), therefore in Latin, you just use an adj: omnis civitas

Genitive of description

Usually composed of a noun + adj

"vir magnae sapientiae → a man of great wisdom → a very wise man"

Subjective/objective genitive

When two things are in genitive (usually the first one is the object and the subject one is in adj form)

"severitatis invidiae → hatred of severity"

Dative

Dative of Agent

Used w gerundive to indicate upon whom the obligation lies

"haec tibi agenda sunt → these things are to be done by you" or "these things are for you to do"

Dative of Possession

Used with esse (sometimes the esse is implied)

"Tantaene animis caelestibus irae"
"est mihi domi pater → I have a father at home (there is a father to me at home)"

Dative of Reference

Usual dative

Dative of purpose

"locum castris deligit → he selects a location for the camp"
"rei publicae cladi sunt → They are ruin to the state (they are for a disaster to the state)"

Accusative

Accusative of place to which

"Veronam venis? → are you coming to Verona?"

Accusative of time duration

"multos annos regnavit → he ruled for many years"
"decem dies manebit → he will stay for ten days"

Ablative

Ablative of cause

Usually without prep (or ex or de)

"ex ira clamavit → he shouted out of anger"
"timore tremit → he trembles from fear"

Ablative of agent

By whom (passive verb, a/ab, ablative) (means has no a/ab and is usually a tool)

Ablative of time when

No prep, ordinal numbers

"tertia hora venit → he came at the third hour"

Ablative of time within which

No prep, usually cardinal numbers

"paucis horis discedent → they will depart within a few hours"

Ablative of comparison

No quam; but when using quam, both nouns have the same case

"marcus est altior Paulo → Marcus is taller than Paul"
"Marcus est altior quam Paulus"

Ablative of separation

Can be w or wout prep; more abstract, talking about freeing or lacking: physical or abstract act of separation

Ablative of place from which

Uses ex/e/a/ab/de

Ablative of respect

(Distinguished in piety)

Ablative of place where

Preposition + non-motion verb

"maneo in Africa → I am staying in Africa"

Ablative of means

Accompanies a verb

"pedibus → on foot"
"equis → by horse"

Ablative of instrument

Usually a tool

"gladio → with a sword"

Ablative of manner

In what way?

"Magna voce → in a loud voice"

Ablative of quality

Describes the noun

"summa virtute → of the highest courage"

Locative

Form looks genitive or ablative, answers "where?"

Usually the same as the gen sg; for plural nouns (1 and 2) same form as abl pl; special forms: domus→domi; rus→ruri.

Advanced Grammar Concepts

Conditionals

Subjunctive

Past Contrary to Fact

Pluperfect subjunctive (translate with had and would have)

"Si id fecisset, prudens fuisset" → "if he had done this, he would have been wise"
Present Contrary to Fact

Imperfect subjunctive (translate with were and would (be))

"si id facerent, prudens esset" → "if he were doing this, he would be wise"
Future Less Vivid (should would)

Present subjunctive (translate with should and would)

"si id faciat, prudens sit" → "if he should do this, he would be wise"

Indicative

Simple Fact Past

Past (either imperfect or perfect) indicative

"si id fecit, prudens fuit" → "If he did this, he was wise"
Simple Fact Present

Present indicative

"si id facit, prudens est" → "if he is doing this, he is wise"
Future More Vivid

Future indicative (can also be future perfect)

"si id faciet, prudens erit" → "if he does (will do) this, he will be wise"

Key Latin Language Conventions

Indirect Statement

With head verbs (saying thinking, knowing, perceiving, feeling, seeing hearing, etc.) and infinitives (tense is relative) (accusative subject)

Ablative Absolute

Noun/pronoun with modifying ablative participle (self-contained, independent from main clause)

Gerund

Verbal noun (only 4th principal part neuter singular (gen, dat, acc, abl))

Amor legendi "love of reading"

Gerundive

Verbal adjective 4th principal part (-ndus, -nda, -ndum) (denotes necessity or obligation)

Liber legendus "a book (about) to be read"

After a verb like give, deliver, have, receive, demand, undertake, the gerundive describes a purpose:

Aedem Castoris habuit tuendam "he had the temple of Castor to take care of"

Gerund-Gerundive Flip

Latin prefers not to use gerunds for direct objects. The gerund-gerundive flip can happen in any case where the gerund would have taken a direct object. Latin prefers to flip to a gerundive phrase that agrees with the object.

Acc with ad (purpose): ad vicendum hostes (for conquering the enemies) → ad hostes vicendos (for the enemies to be conquered)
Gen expressing purpose, cause, or description: ars scribendi libros (the art of writing books) → ars librorum scribendorum (the art of books to be written)
Dat (purpose): studium legendi libros (enthusiasm for reading books) → studium libris legendis (enthusiasm for books to be read)
Abl (means, cause, manner): in discendo linguas (in learning languages) → in discendis linguis (in languages to be learned)

Supine

4th principal part stem (-um (acc)/ -u (abl)):

Accusative: verbs of motion indicating purpose (to _)

"persuasum amicis venerunt → they came to persuade the friends"

Ablative: used with neuter of adjectives (to _)

"mirabile dictu → amazing to say"

Hortatory

Independent subjunctive, same as jussive in construction, but only 1st plural

Jussive

Independent subjunctive (main verb is subjunctive)

Deliberative Question

Independent subjunctive: subjunctive (can also be indicative) question, expressing doubt, indignation, or impossibility, as if asking the speaker's own advice. (translation: interrogative+auxiliary+inf (can use shall/should, can/could))

"quid agam → what am i to do?"

Potential Subjunctive

Independent subjunctive: suggests an action as conceivable/possible subjunctive. In an independent clause (pres/ft: refer to the future; impf: to the past; plup (rare): what might have happened; should, would, may, might + tense indicator

"velim sic existimes → I should wish you to think thus"

Indirect Command

Dependent subjunctive: subjunctive clause introduced by ut/ne with command-ish verbs (translate verb in the clause as an infinitive)

"imperat vobis ut hoc faciatis → he commands you (pl) to do this"

Indirect Question

Dependent subjunctive: indicative discourse verbs, subjunctive clause with interrogative word (IC, interrogative, DC)

"rogant quid Gaius faciat → They ask what Gaius is doing"

Purpose

Dependent subjunctive: subjunctive clause introduced by ut/ne (in order to infinitive or use may/might)

Result

Dependent subjunctive: ut (not ne), adverb (ita sic tam adeo), adjective (tantus); translated with no auxiliaries

Cum Clauses

Cum temporal clause: indicative (when..) can also be circumstantial

Causal clause: subjunctive + causal relationship (since…)

Concessive clause: subjunctive + tamen (although…nevertheless)

Proviso Clause

dummodo (+ne): (provided that…)

Fear Clause

vereor, metuo, and timeo (ut and ne are inverted): will/may (present); would/might (imperfect)

"timeo ne id credant → I fear that they will believe this"
"vereor ut id credant → I fear that they may not believe this"

Relative Clause of Description

Agrees with antecedent in gender and number with no forms that indicate otherwise

Relative Clause of Characteristic

Relative pronoun, subjunctive verb in clause, general, indefinite, interrogative, negative antecedent (nemo, quis, etc.)

"The sort of/ the kind of" to indicate an indefinite antecedent

"would" for the subjunctive verb

"Hic est liber quem omnes legant → This is the kind of book that all would read"
"Quis est qui huic credat → Who is there who (is of such a kind that he) would trust this man?"
